	\section*{Realistic Projectile Motion}
	\section{Introduction}
	Consider two identical objects that start at the same height above the ground. One object is dropped, and the other is launched horizontally. 
	\\ \indent Including air resistance, we quantify which object hits the ground first. This paper explores the dependence of landing time difference on the parameters mass, starting height, and launch velocity.
	\section{Methods}
	Without drag, the equations of motion for a projectile can be modeled as four differential equations:
	\[\frac{dx}{dt} = v, \qquad \frac{dv_{x}}{dt} = 0\]
	\[\frac{dy}{dt} = v, \qquad \frac{dv_{y}}{dt} = -g\]
	However, factoring in drag, we have two components to consider:
			\[F_{D,y} = \frac{v_{y}}{v} F_{D} \to -v_{y}vB_{2}\]
			\[F_{D,x} = \frac{v_{x}}{v} F_{D} \to -v_{x}vB_{2}\]
	These forces then act and update the $x$ and $y$ velocities, \\
	giving rise to four equations of motion in Euler's method: 
	\begin{eqnarray}
		x_{i+1}=x_i+v_{x,i}\Delta t\\
		y_{i+1}=y_i+v_{y,i}\Delta t\\
		v_{x,i+1} = v_{x,i}-\frac{B_2}{m}v_iv_{x,i}\Delta t\\
		v_{y,i+1}=v_{y,i}-\left(g+\frac{B_2}{m}v_iv_{y,i}\right)\Delta t
	\end{eqnarray}
	\indent where $B_2= \frac{1}{2}c\rho A$.
	\\\indent The parameters $c, \rho, A$ correspond to the drag coefficient, air density, and cross-sectional area. 
	\\ \indent With these, there is no analytic solution to the equations of motion, and so numerical methods must be used.
	\\\\\indent The standard Euler's method is used, where the four first-order differential equations are iterated through as shown in Algorithm 1.
	\\\\
		\begin{minipage}{1\linewidth}
		\rule{\linewidth}{0.5pt}
		\refstepcounter{algorithm}
		\textbf{Algorithm 1} Euler's Method
	
		\vspace{-0.5em}
		\rule{\linewidth}{0.5pt}
		
		\begin{algorithmic}
			\Require $y>0$
			\Ensure time array $t$
			\State $t\gets[~]$\Comment{Initialize empty list}
			\State $(x, y, v_x, v_y,v) \gets \text{Initial Values}$
			\State $i\gets0$
			\Repeat
				\State $x_{i+1}=x_i+v_{x,i}\Delta t$
				\State $y_{i+1}=y_i+v_{y,i}\Delta t$
				\State $v_{x,i+1} = v_{x,i}-B_2v_iv_{x,i}\Delta t/m$
				\State $v_{y,i+1}=v_{y,i}-\left(g+B_2v_iv_{y,i}\right)\Delta t/m$
				\State $t_{i+1}\gets t_i+\Delta t$
				\State $i\gets i+1$
			\Until{$y_i<0$}
			\State \Return $t$
		\end{algorithmic}		
		\rule{\linewidth}{0.5pt}
		\vspace{0.25mm}
	\end{minipage}
	\\\indent We want to quantify the time difference between the two objects hitting the ground. Our numerical method cannot find the value when height exactly equals zero, so we must interpolate between the last and second-to-last points. For the shot object, we interpolate between the final two $x$-positions.
	\\\\
	\begin{minipage}{1\linewidth}
		\rule{\linewidth}{0.5pt}
		\refstepcounter{algorithm}
		\textbf{Algorithm 2} Shot Landing Time Interpolation
		
		\vspace{-0.5em}
		\rule{\linewidth}{0.5pt}
		
		\begin{algorithmic}
			\Require $y_{n-1}>0,y_n<0$
			\Ensure Landing Time $t_L$
			\State $t_L\gets[~]$\Comment{Initialize empty list}
			\State $x_{L}\gets\dfrac{x_{n}-x_{n-1}}{|y_n+y_{n-1}|}y_{n-1}+x_{n-1}$
			\State $t_L\gets t_{n-1}+\dfrac{x_L-x_{n-1}}{v_{x,n-2}}$
			\State \Return $t_L$
		\end{algorithmic}		
		\rule{\linewidth}{0.5pt}
		\vspace{0.25mm}
	\end{minipage}
	\\ \indent For the dropped object, we extrapolate from the latest positive height to the ground.
	\[t_{L,drop}=t_{n-1}+|\dfrac{y_{n-1}}{v_{y,n-1}}|\]
	From here, we explore the dependence of the difference in landing times $\Delta t_L=t_{L,shot}-t_{L,drop}$ on the parameters $m,y_0,v_{x,0}$, by generating initial values ranging from 10 to 10000 for each control parameter.
	\\ \indent The baseline object was a sphere:
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item $m=100$ kg
		\item $y_0 = 10000$ m
		\item $v_{x,0} = 100$ m/s (0 for dropped object)
		\item $A=0.1\text{ m}^2$
		\item $\rho=1.225\text{ kg/m}^3$
		\item $c=0.5$
	\end{enumerate}
	\indent No generative AI was used in this assignment.

	\section{Discussion}
	All control parameters were found to have a dwindling effect on landing time difference in the extreme limits ($\sim$10,000). This agrees with the numerical model shown in Section II.
	\subsection{Initial Height $y_0$}
	With no ground, both objects have identical paths of motion as their initial height changes. As time passes, the shot object reaches the ground slightly later than the dropped object. At small height, the objects reach the ground at nearly the same time, but we do not see any interesting effects or consequences from the landing time depending on the height. However, increasing the starting height clearly forces the objects to spend longer in free fall - therefore slowly increasing the separation. However, the objects keep accelerating. At large heights, a small change in height barely changes the Landing Time Difference ($\Delta T_L$), as the extra height doesn't allow for a (proportionally) large space to accelerate. Furthermore, The objects reach terminal velocity, where their y-velocity does not increase any further. This is shown in Equation (4), where the term $g+\dfrac{B_2}{m}v_iv_{y,i}$ goes to zero, and both objects approach zero acceleration.
